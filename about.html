Why this

Software ships in seconds. Hardware ships next week.

I've written production firmware that went into shipping radar hardware, so I've lived both loops. Software is tight: change it, build it, see it, fix it — minutes. Hardware isn't. You send a board to a fab, wait out the week, find the one mistake, and start the clock over.

That gap — between how fast you can think and how slowly you can hold the result in your hand — is the thing I want to close. PrometheusPCB is my answer to it: pull fabrication onto the bench, so the wait stops being the bottleneck and your ideas start being the bottleneck again.

Why me

I build things that have to physically work — and I finish them.

My training is split on purpose. Electrical and mechanical engineering, because a machine like this lives right at the seam between the two — precise motion, controlled materials, real electronics, all in one box.

Before this I shipped production firmware at a radar company. On my own, I've launched live products that people actually use and validated a consumer startup with real customers in the field. The throughline is simple: I'd rather build the real thing than talk about it, and I finish what I start. A desktop ceramic fab is a hard, physical, defensible problem — which is exactly why I picked it over something easier.

Why now

The pieces finally line up.

Low-temperature co-fired ceramic and direct ink writing are both mature enough to build a real machine around — the materials science isn't the open question anymore, the machine is. And ceramic doesn't just carry the traces; it carries the passives. LTCC has buried resistors, capacitors, and inductors inside its layers in production for decades — which is what lets a desktop machine fab most of a board's parts instead of just its wiring. Meanwhile the only tools doing true multi-layer additive electronics today are six-figure closed boxes, and everyone else is still mailing designs to a fab. There's a gap exactly where a desktop, open machine should be.

Texas Tech's Innovation Hub and an NSF I-Corps grant gave me the room to start chasing that gap now, instead of someday. The technical brief lays out the architecture and the one materials problem the whole thing turns on.

What's next

PrometheusPCB is pre-prototype and moving fast. I'm building toward the first machine, talking to the hardware teams and labs who'd use it, and looking for a co-founder on the materials side — someone who reads the hard problem and gets excited instead of nervous.

If that's you, or if you just want a ceramic fab on your bench someday, let's talk.
